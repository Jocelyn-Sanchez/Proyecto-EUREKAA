Second Law of Newton

F = ma
"The acceleration of a body is directly proportional to the force applied to that body. In addition, the change in the body's velocity will occur along the line of action of the applied force."

The Second Law of Newton, or Fundamental Principle of Dynamics, is the second of the theoretical postulates formulated by the British scientist Sir Isaac Newton (1642-1727), based on previous studies by Galileo Galilei and René Descartes. Like his Law of Inertia, it was published in 1684 in his work Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy, one of the fundamental works of modern physics. The law states in his original Latin text: “Mutationem motus proportionalem esse vi motrici impressæ, & fieri secundum lineam rectam qua vis illa imprimitur” which means: “The change of motion is proportional to the motive force impressed and takes place along the straight line in which that force is impressed.” This means that the acceleration a given body experiences is proportional to the force applied to it, which may or may not be constant. The essence of this law is understanding that force is the cause of changes in motion and velocity.

The Second Law of Newton of Motion, also known as the Fundamental Principle of Dynamics, establishes the crucial relationship among the force acting on an object, its mass, and the resulting acceleration. Unlike the first law, which describes what happens when there is no net force, the second explains what happens when an unbalanced net force exists: the object changes its velocity, meaning it accelerates. The law can be summarized in the famous mathematical equation: (F = m · a). In this formula, F represents the net force (the sum of all vector forces acting on the body), m is the mass of the object, and a is the acceleration it experiences.
This relationship implies two key proportionalities:

  • Direct proportionality between force and acceleration: The greater the net force applied to an object, the greater its acceleration. If we double the force, we double the acceleration, assuming mass remains constant.
  • Inverse proportionality between mass and acceleration: The greater the mass of an object (its inertia), the smaller the acceleration produced by the same applied force. A much greater force is needed to move heavier objects with the same acceleration as lighter ones.
The Second Law of Newton is essential for measuring and calculating forces in the International System of Units. One Newton (N) is defined as the force required to produce an acceleration of 1 meter per second squared (1 m/s²) on a mass of 1 kilogram (1 kg).
