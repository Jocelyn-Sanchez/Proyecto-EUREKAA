Magnetism

What is magnetism?

Magnetism is a fundamental interaction of nature that arises from the presence of moving electric charges and the quantum properties of subatomic particles. It manifests as forces of attraction and repulsion between materials, as well as the generation of invisible fields capable of influencing matter at a distance. On a physical level, magnetism is an inseparable part of electromagnetism, mathematically described by Maxwell’s equations. These principles establish that electric and magnetic fields are not separate entities, but rather different aspects of the same phenomenon that propagates through space in the form of electromagnetic waves, which include light, radio waves, X-rays, and many others. Magnetism exists on both microscopic scales — in the spins of electrons — and colossal scales, such as the Earth's magnetic field, solar magnetism, or the fields generated in galaxies and galaxy clusters. Thanks to magnetism, essential technologies such as motors, generators, transformers, hard drives, sensors, satellites, and navigation systems function.

Microscopic origin of magnetism

The deeper origin of magnetism lies in the structure of the atom. Electrons have a quantum property called spin, which acts as a tiny magnet with its own magnetic moment. In addition, the orbital motion of each electron around the nucleus also generates a magnetic field. The combination of these two effects determines how atoms interact with external fields. In many atoms, electrons group into pairs with opposite spins, canceling their magnetic moments. However, in some elements such as iron, nickel, or cobalt, unpaired electrons remain whose magnetic moments align collectively, allowing the existence of ferromagnetism. Quantum physics explains that alignment occurs when electrons of different atoms interact through what is known as “quantum exchange,” a phenomenon that cannot be described with classical physics but is crucial to understanding why a material can become a permanent magnet.

Types of magnetic behavior

  • Diamagnetism: All materials exhibit it to some degree. It arises when an external field induces microscopic currents that generate an opposing field. It is extremely weak but universal, even observed in materials such as water or copper.
  • Paramagnetism: Occurs in materials whose atoms have magnetic moments that do not completely cancel. In the presence of a field, these moments tend to align, increasing the field’s intensity. Paramagnetic materials include aluminum, sodium, and liquid oxygen, which displays very notable paramagnetic effects due to its molecular structure.
  • Ferromagnetism: Characterized by the formation of magnetic domains, regions where the magnetic moments of billions of atoms are aligned. The energy required to maintain this order is very low, allowing the existence of permanent magnets. Ferromagnetism is crucial for industry, digital storage, and the manufacturing of motors.
  • Antiferromagnetism: In these materials, magnetic moments align in opposite directions, canceling each other out. Although externally they appear non-magnetic, they are very important in spintronics, sensors, and advanced memories such as MRAM.
  • Ferrimagnetism: Similar to antiferromagnetism but with unequal magnetic moments, producing a resulting field. Ferrites are widely used in antennas, transformers, wireless chargers, and radio-frequency devices.

Fundamental laws of magnetism

The mathematical description of magnetism was developed over centuries and consolidated in the 19th century through the work of Maxwell, Ampère, Faraday, and Biot–Savart. Each of these laws explains both the generation of magnetic fields and their interaction with moving charges and materials.

  • Biot–Savart Law: describes how an electric current produces a magnetic field in space. It is essential for calculating the field around wires, coils, and solenoids, enabling the design of motors, superconducting magnets, and transformers.
  • Ampère’s Law: states that the magnetic field around a conductor is proportional to the current intensity. In its Maxwell-extended version it includes the concept of displacement current, key to understanding how light propagates in a vacuum.
  • Faraday’s Law: a change in magnetic flux produces an electromotive force (voltage). It is the basis of electrical generation, from small dynamos to giant hydroelectric or wind turbines.
  • Lorentz Force Law: explains the force experienced by a moving charge within a magnetic field. This law allows the operation of electric motors, particle accelerators, and plasma confinement devices such as tokamaks.
  • Maxwell’s Equations: unify electricity and magnetism into a single theoretical framework. They also predict the existence of electromagnetic waves that travel at the speed of light, demonstrating that light is a manifestation of electromagnetism.

Magnetism on Earth (geomagnetism)

The Earth's magnetic field originates in the outer core, a layer of liquid iron and nickel that moves by convection due to the planet’s internal heat. This movement, combined with Earth’s rotation, generates a dynamo effect that produces a global magnetic field extending tens of thousands of kilometers into space. Geomagnetism protects Earth from the solar wind, a constant flow of charged particles emitted by the Sun. Without this natural shield, the atmosphere would have been eroded, as happened on Mars billions of years ago according to studies by NASA and ESA. In addition, geomagnetism enables navigation using compasses, influences animal migration, and is essential to space weather studies, which examine how solar variations affect Earth.

Associated phenomena

  • Magnetosphere: A theoretical region surrounding the planet that deflects much of the solar wind. It has an elongated shape due to the pressure of the solar particle flow and is the site of complex processes such as magnetic reconnection.
  • Auroras: The northern and southern lights occur when solar particles penetrate the magnetosphere and collide with oxygen and nitrogen molecules in the atmosphere. These collisions excite the atoms, which release light when they return to their normal state, producing the luminous displays typical of polar regions.
  • Magnetic reversals: Throughout Earth’s history, the north and south magnetic poles have swapped positions many times. The geological record shows hundreds of reversals, studied through paleomagnetism, which help scientists understand core dynamics and plate tectonics.
  • Anomalies: There are regions where the magnetic field is weaker or stronger than usual. The most studied is the South Atlantic Anomaly, where the magnetosphere is closer to the surface, affecting low-orbit satellites and requiring temporary shutdowns of sensitive instruments.

Magnetism in the Sun and space

The Sun is a gigantic natural laboratory of magnetic physics. Its magnetic field is thousands of times stronger than Earth’s in certain regions and is organized in complex structures such as coronal loops, filaments, and sunspots. The twisting and reconnection of field lines produce explosive phenomena such as solar flares and coronal mass ejections, capable of releasing massive amounts of energy in minutes. These events are studied by NASA, ESA, and the Solar and Heliospheric Observatory (SOHO), as they can affect communication systems, GPS, satellites, and Earth’s power grids. On cosmic scales, magnetic fields play roles in star formation, galaxy dynamics, and relativistic jets from black holes.

Measurement of magnetism

The unit of measurement for magnetic field strength in the International System is the tesla (T), although the gauss is also used in astronomical and historical contexts. Earth's magnetic field ranges between 25 and 65 microteslas, while a common magnet can exceed 0.1 teslas, and superconducting magnets used in magnetic resonance imaging can reach 3 to 7 teslas. Precise measurement of magnetism is carried out using a variety of specialized instruments:

  • Proton magnetometers: used in geophysics to measure the field with great stability and precision.
  • SQUID sensors: superconducting devices capable of detecting extremely weak fields, even those of biological origin.
  • Fluxgate magnetometers: used in space satellites, such as ESA’s Swarm mission, to map the Earth’s magnetic field.
  • Magnetoresistive sensors: widely used in electronics, digital compasses, and mobile phones.

History of magnetism

The history of magnetism begins with the discovery of magnetite in ancient Greece and China, where minerals capable of attracting iron were first observed. For centuries, the compass enabled the advancement of navigation and the exploration of the world. However, it was not until the Modern Age that the phenomenon began to be understood scientifically.

  • William Gilbert (1600): considered the father of modern magnetism, described Earth as a giant magnet.
  • Ørsted (1820): accidentally discovered the relationship between electric current and magnetic field, initiating the era of electromagnetism.
  • Faraday (1831): demonstrated electromagnetic induction, the basis for the operation of generators and motors.
  • Maxwell (1864): unified electricity and magnetism in his equations, revolutionizing modern physics.

Applications of magnetism

Magnetism has essential applications in practically all areas of science, technology, and modern life. From electrical generation to advanced medicine, its influence is deep and everyday. Without it, it would not be possible to produce energy on a large scale, store digital information, orient satellites, or obtain medical images of internal organs without radiation.

  • Electric motors: use magnetic forces to convert electricity into motion.
  • Electric generators: convert mechanical energy into electrical energy through induction.
  • Maglev trains: magnetic levitation reduces friction, allowing speeds above 500 km/h.
  • Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI): uses superconducting magnets to visualize internal tissues without affecting health.
  • Digital storage: hard drives and magnetic tapes depend on the magnetic orientation of millions of domains.
  • Navigation: magnetic sensors allow orientation in phones, drones, and GPS systems.
  • Industrial electromagnets: used in cranes, material separation, and heavy robotics.

Current problems and modern research

Modern research in magnetism covers very broad fields, from quantum physics to astrophysics. Space agencies and research centers continuously monitor solar activity and Earth’s magnetic field because of the importance of space weather for modern society. Induced currents from solar storms can damage transformers, interrupt communications, and affect satellites. Scientific missions such as Swarm (ESA) and DSCOVR (NOAA–NASA) provide precise data on changes in Earth’s magnetism. Technologically, research in “spintronics” seeks to manipulate electron spin to create faster and more efficient devices than those based on electric current.

  • Swarm (ESA): satellites specialized in mapping Earth’s magnetic field with unprecedented precision.
  • World Magnetic Model (NOAA–British Geological Survey): used worldwide in air navigation, marine navigation, and GPS systems.
  • Internal dynamo: geophysical research aims to understand how Earth’s core evolves and its possible future changes.
  • Magnetic reconnection: fundamental process in plasma physics that explains solar explosions and intense auroras.

Conclusion

Magnetism is a fundamental piece for understanding how the universe works, from the internal structure of atoms to the largest cosmic processes. Its presence on Earth ensures protection from the solar wind, regulates atmospheric phenomena, and enables navigation and life as we know it. At the same time, its study has driven technological advances that sustain the modern world: electric motors, storage devices, advanced transportation systems, medical equipment, and communication technologies.

Current research continues to reveal new aspects of magnetism, both in materials and in solar and space plasmas. From scientific satellites monitoring the magnetic field, to particle accelerators and research in spintronics and superconductivity, magnetism remains a key area for science and engineering. Understanding it allows not only explaining natural phenomena but also developing more efficient, safe, and sustainable technologies for the future.
